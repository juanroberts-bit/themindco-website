The Context

The AI employment debate
is asking the wrong question

Clients, colleagues, and partners constantly ask me the same question: will AI replace human work? It is a legitimate concern — but it frames the issue incorrectly. Every major technological wave in history has displaced specific jobs while simultaneously creating entirely new categories of demand for human effort.

We are not entering an unprecedented era of displacement. We are entering a familiar pattern, playing out at a faster pace and broader scope than before. The question worth asking is not whether AI will take jobs. It is: which capabilities, roles, and business models will capture the new work that AI-driven efficiency will release?

The Core Concept

The Jevons Paradox:
efficiency multiplies demand

In 1865, British economist William Stanley Jevons made a counterintuitive observation: as steam engines became more efficient, coal consumption increased rather than decreased. More efficient technology lowered the cost per unit of output — which expanded demand far beyond what the efficiency gain saved.

The same mechanism operates in labor markets. When AI makes a task cheaper or faster, it does not simply reduce the need for that task. It lowers the barrier to doing more of it, opens new adjacent demand, and creates new roles to design, validate, integrate, and operate the new system.

Source: Illustrative model based on Jevons (1865) and labor economics literature. S2 reflects post-AI efficiency gain.
What Jevons observed
More efficient machines → lower cost per unit → higher total consumption → more workers needed, not fewer.
What this means for AI
More capable AI → lower cost per task → expanded demand for decisions, validation, and new use cases → new human roles.
The Real Pattern

Down first.
Then up.

The Jevons effect does not happen instantly. Each technological wave follows a predictable arc: an initial displacement phase where specific roles are eliminated faster than new ones emerge, followed by an inflection point, followed by a net creation phase where the efficiency-driven expansion of demand outpaces the original displacement.

Goldman Sachs research on prior automation cycles suggests the displacement phase typically lasts 18–24 months before the net job creation phase begins. Understanding which phase you are in matters as much as understanding the long-run direction.

Strategic Implications

What business leaders
should do differently

If the Jevons pattern holds — and the evidence strongly suggests it will — the strategic imperative for organizations is not to minimize AI adoption to protect headcount. It is to actively position for the creation phase by building the capabilities, roles, and operating models that the expansion of AI-driven demand will require.

Stop asking "will AI take jobs?"
Start asking which new roles will emerge in design, validation, integration, governance, and human-AI collaboration — and build toward them.
Invest during the displacement phase
The organizations that build AI-adjacent capabilities during the displacement dip will be best positioned to capture the Jevons creation surge.
Identify your elasticity
Not every sector rebounds equally. Identify where AI-driven efficiency creates adjacent demand in your specific market — before competitors do.
Redesign for amplification
Redesign roles for human-AI complementarity. The most valuable employees will be those who amplify AI outputs, not those who compete with AI inputs.
"Efficiency does not reduce human work.
It reinvents and expands it."
Final Thoughts
The question was never
whether.
It was always
how fast — and
who would be ready.

Every major technology in history has been met with legitimate anxiety about what it would do to human work. Every time, the long-run answer has been net expansion — but only for those who adapted their capabilities, business models, and organizational structures to the new paradigm fast enough to benefit from the Jevons rebound.

AI is not different. The organizations that will thrive are not those that resist the wave, but those that build the capacity to surf it — capturing the expanded demand that efficiency always, eventually, creates.
